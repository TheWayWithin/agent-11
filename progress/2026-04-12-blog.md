# The platform that doesn't want your hook

The wip.co post is seven words. "Added wip output to /dailyreport #agent11." No hook. No thread. No "what I learnt" beat. Just what I shipped, tagged with the project.

I added wip.co as a fourth output to both `/dailyreport` and `/blog` today. Twitter, LinkedIn, wip. Three social platforms, three completely different shapes. Twitter wants a hook in 280 characters. LinkedIn wants a 140-character teaser that survives the "see more" fold, then a professional insight ending in a question. wip wants a changelog line.

The changelog line is the hardest to get wrong and the easiest to get right. You can't dress it up. It starts with a verb — "Shipped", "Fixed", "Deployed" — and ends with a hashtag. The format won't let you hide behind a clever hook or a well-structured narrative. Either you did the thing or you didn't.

That's the design. wip.co is a community of people tracking streaks. You post what you finished. The social pressure is "did you ship today?", not "did you write something clever today?". The format matches the incentive: short, honest, done.

For `/dailyreport`, the implementation generates one post per milestone from the raw daily report. If I shipped three things, three wip posts. Each one a single sentence, under 150 characters, past-tense verb, project hashtag at the end. The hashtag auto-derives from whatever's in CLAUDE.md — "AGENT-11" becomes `#agent11` — or you set `WIP_PROJECT_HASHTAG` in your env to override it.

For `/blog`, it's one post per blog: either framed as a completed action ("Deleted 600 lines of Python from /dailyreport #agent11") or as a publication announcement ("Published 'Why was I calling OpenAI' #agent11"), depending on whether the blog describes work done or an opinion held.

Both share the same voice guide, the same env config, the same file structure. The only difference is what goes in the post.
